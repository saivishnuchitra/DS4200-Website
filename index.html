Mapping the Aging Brain

Subtitle: MRI-Based Analysis of Cognitive and Structural Changes in Dementia
Team: Wali Qureshi, Gianni Diarbi and Sai Vishnu Chitra
Course: DS4200 Information Presentation and Data Visualization

Introduction

Our project, Mapping the Aging Brain: MRI-Based Analysis of Cognitive and Structural Changes in Dementia, focuses on understanding how the human brain evolves with age and how these changes relate to memory, thinking ability, and neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer’s. Using the Open Access Series of Imaging Studies (OASIS) dataset, we analyze MRI-based measurements like brain volume alongside cognitive scores, age, education, and gender to uncover meaningful patterns across different populations.

The goal of our project is to identify relationships between brain structure and cognitive decline, helping visualize how aging and dementia affect the brain over time. Our analysis involves preprocessing the data - handling missing values, normalizing MRI metrics, and encoding categorical variables - to ensure accuracy and consistency. From there, we explore trends such as the correlation between age and brain atrophy, as well as differences in cognitive performance across dementia and non-dementia groups.

To bring our findings to life, we have created both static and interactive visualizations, including scatterplots, distribution graphs, and 3D brain diagrams that illustrate structural decline and its cognitive impact. Ultimately, our project aims to combine neuroscience and data analytics to make complex information about the aging brain more accessible and insightful for researchers, clinicians, and the public.

The Data

Our project uses data from the Open Access Series of Imaging Studies (OASIS), a publicly available MRI database developed by the Washington University Alzheimer’s Disease Research Center and collaborators at Harvard University and the Biomedical Informatics Research Network. The dataset provides high-quality brain imaging data that supports research on aging, cognition, and Alzheimer’s disease. We accessed this data via Kaggle.

We draw from two related datasets: OASIS Cross-Sectional and OASIS Longitudinal. The cross-sectional dataset contains 416 participants aged 18 to 96, each with 3–4 MRI scans collected during a single session. It includes demographic and clinical information such as age, gender, years of education, cognitive test scores (MMSE), dementia rating (CDR), and MRI-derived brain volume measurements. The longitudinal dataset follows 150 older adults aged 60 to 96 over several years, totaling 373 imaging sessions. Participants were scanned two or more times to track structural and cognitive changes over time - some remaining nondemented, while others progressed from healthy to Alzheimer’s diagnosis.

Together, these datasets provide nearly 800 total MRI sessions, offering a rich foundation for exploring how brain structure, cognition, and dementia progression are interconnected.

Summary

Across our six visualizations, several clear and meaningful patterns emerge about how aging and dementia shape the brain. First, we observe a strong and consistent negative relationship between age and normalized whole-brain volume, showing that brain atrophy is a universal part of aging—but it progresses faster and is more pronounced in individuals with dementia. Demented participants not only start with lower brain volume but also show a steeper decline over time. When examining cognitive performance, non-demented individuals cluster tightly at high MMSE scores regardless of brain volume, whereas people with dementia show substantial variability and a clear link between lower brain volume and reduced cognitive function. Age distributions further highlight that dementia is overwhelmingly concentrated in older populations. Boxplots and age-group comparisons reinforce that across every age bracket, the dementia group consistently has lower brain volume than their non-demented peers. Finally, the correlation heatmaps reveal stable trends across subgroups—especially the strong negative association between age and brain volume and a positive association between education, SES, and cognitive performance—while also showing that the link between brain volume and MMSE is strongest within the dementia cohort, where structural decline is most tied to functional impairment.

Looking ahead, these findings point to several areas for future exploration. Incorporating longitudinal MRI scans would allow us to model individual trajectories of brain atrophy over time, providing a dynamic picture rather than a cross-sectional snapshot. Additional predictors like lifestyle factors, genetic markers (e.g., APOE status), or cardiovascular health could deepen our understanding of what accelerates or protects against cognitive decline. Machine learning models could be trained to identify early indicators of dementia risk by combining structural MRI metrics with demographic and cognitive variables. Finally, expanding our interactive visualizations—such as adding drill-downs by gender, education level, or socioeconomic status—could make the tool even more useful for researchers and clinicians who need to explore patterns at multiple levels of detail. Collectively, the visualizations show that age-related brain changes are measurable, interpretable, and strongly tied to cognitive outcomes, reinforcing the importance of early detection and ongoing research into the structural roots of dementia.
